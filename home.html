05
Prompt Design

How I
Prompt

My approach to prompt engineering comes directly from annotation guideline writing — both are exercises in specifying intent precisely enough that a system can act on it without ambiguity.

At Accenture, I learned that quality failures in AI outputs almost always trace back to under-specified instructions. A prompt is no different from an annotation standard: explicit exclusion criteria reduce ambiguity more effectively than positive descriptions alone.

The case studies below document real iteration loops — what failed, what changed, and why the refined output worked better.

  • 01
    Context anchoring — embedding prompts inside a named framework tells the model it's operating within defined rules, not generating freely.
  • 02
    Constraint as direction — "avoid X" is a more precise instruction than "be Y." Exclusion criteria sharpen outputs faster than positive descriptions.
  • 03
    Character specificity creates systemic specificity — defining a narrator's concrete history forces the model to generate a specific world, not a generic one.
  • 04
    Format-to-UI alignment — specifying output format to match an existing design template eliminates post-generation editing entirely.
Case 01 — Y.E.E.T. Faction Dialogue: Corporate Voice
NASA Studio Project · Spring 2025
Version 1 — Weak Prompt
Write dialogue for a corporate character in a space debris game.
Version 3 — Refined Prompt
You are writing dialogue for AXION Corp's orbital salvage lead in Y.E.E.T., a near-future space governance game. AXION treats debris as a revenue stream, not a hazard. Their language is corporate, optimistic about extraction, and dismissive of regulatory frameworks. Write 3 lines of in-game dialogue this character would say when defending their right to operate in contested orbital zones. Keep scientific terminology accurate to Low Earth Orbit operations. Each line under 25 words.
Improvements: Named faction identity Ideological constraint Domain accuracy anchor Format spec: 25 words Quantity control
Case 02 — Extra Omnes: Post-Labor Vignette
Parsons Thesis · Fall 2024
Version 1 — Weak Prompt
Write a story about a future where robots do all the work.
Version 3 — Refined Prompt
Context: This is a vignette for "Extra Omnes," a speculative policy project exploring a post-labor economy (2045–2060). The world operates on a Time-as-Currency exchange system. Write a 200-word first-person narrative from a former logistics manager (30s, India-born, relocated to Amsterdam) in her first year navigating the Time Currency system. Show the friction of redefining value when identity was built on productive output. Avoid: dystopian framing, utopian resolution. Tone: unsettled, precise, quietly political. End on ambiguity, not conclusion.
Improvements: World-state context block Character specificity Explicit exclusion criteria Tone & register spec Structural end-state control
Case 02 — Output Comparison
Why the refined prompt worked
V1 Output
In the year 2075, robots had taken over every job. Humans were free to do whatever they wanted. Some were happy, but others felt lost without purpose. John, a former factory worker, sat in his apartment wondering what his life meant now... [Generic narrative. Named male protagonist. Binary emotional arc. Universal moral without nuance.]
V3 Output
The ledger used to make sense. I moved goods. Goods arrived. I was paid. The system had a logic I could trace with my finger. Now I teach a weekend fermentation workshop. Three people came last month. According to the Time Registry, I earned 6.4 credits — enough for two months of transit and a dentist appointment. I do not hate this system. I am learning to not understand it as failure.
Insight: The V3 output required minimal editing. V1 required complete replacement. The work happened in the prompt — not the revision. This is the same principle as annotation guideline design: under-specified instructions produce statistically average outputs.
